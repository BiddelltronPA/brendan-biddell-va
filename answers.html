Brendan Biddell — Straight Answers

The Q&A.

The questions that actually matter for Head of Marketing Programs — martech, data, commercial impact and why Virgin. Answered plainly, no filler.

01

This role is Marketing's voice into Technology. What makes you the translator?

Because I've lived on both sides of that table. At Doltone House I owned the marketing technology roadmap end to end — I scoped what the business needed, translated it into platform, data and integration requirements, then worked with delivery teams to actually ship it.

Most marketers can't spec a platform. Most technologists don't think in customer journeys. I do both — and I make each side feel understood.

My instinct is to start with the commercial outcome, work backwards to the customer journey, and only then decide what the technology has to do. That keeps the roadmap honest — you build what moves the business, not what's shiny.

02

You haven't run AEP or Tealium by name. Why back yourself here?

I'll be direct: I've built the same capability with different logos. Enterprise martech ecosystems, customer data platforms, marketing automation, lifecycle campaigns, dynamic pricing and analytics — I've stood those up at Doltone House and Meriton across HubSpot, Salesforce, GA4, Klaviyo, Shopify and a stack of integration tools.

AEP, Adobe Marketing Cloud and Tealium are the enterprise-grade version of a discipline I already know cold: unifying customer data, segmenting it, and activating it into personalised journeys.

Platforms change. The thinking — data in, insight out, experience delivered — doesn't. And I learn tools fast; my resume is a list of first-time launches.

03

The role calls out AI. Are you actually an AI person, or just fluent in the buzzwords?

Actually an AI person — and I can prove it with things I've built, not tools I've trialled. I've introduced internal LLM tools and custom bots into marketing teams, built live marketing dashboards from scratch, and rebuilt automated email flows that outperformed the HubSpot setup they replaced.

I don't need convincing that AI belongs in the roadmap. I've already been shipping it — and I know the difference between a demo and something that delivers value.

That's the exact instinct this role needs: someone who can separate genuine capability from vendor hype, prioritise the AI investments that move customer experience and commercial outcomes, and actually get them delivered with the technology team.

04

How do you prioritise a technology roadmap when everyone wants everything?

Commercial return and customer impact, ranked ruthlessly. Every initiative has to answer two questions: what does it do for the customer experience, and what does it do for the P&L? If it can't answer both, it waits.

I've built business cases and prioritised marketing spend up to $5M, and I report on it at board level — so I'm comfortable defending a roadmap to executives and saying no to good ideas that aren't the right ones yet.

In an agile delivery environment that means being the person in the room who protects the sequence: quick wins that build trust, foundational data work that unlocks everything else, then the personalisation and loyalty payoffs on top.

05

Virgin has Velocity. What's your read on loyalty and personalisation?

Loyalty is where data stops being a cost centre and starts being a growth engine. I've run loyalty programs at Luna Park, lifecycle CRM across 18 Meriton properties, and segmentation that grew youth visitation 500% and online sales 300%.

A loyalty program isn't a points ledger — it's the richest first-party data asset a brand owns, and the licence to personalise every journey.

With a CDP feeding it, Velocity becomes the connective tissue: one view of the member, personalised across booking, in-flight, on-ground and everything between. That's the difference between "nice" and genuinely better.

06

Why Virgin Australia, and why this role?

Two reasons. First, it's a newly created role with a blank platform roadmap — I do my best work building the engine from scratch, not maintaining someone else's. Every highlight on my CV is a first: first vertical theme park, Sydney's first big Halloween event, a first enterprise martech stack.

Second, Virgin is a brand that has always done things its own way — and I've spent a career on brands people genuinely care about. Aviation is high-volume, data-rich and experience-led, which is exactly the terrain I love.

You want to make flying better than 'nice'. I want to build the technology and data foundation that makes that promise real at every touchpoint.

And practically — I'm ready for Brisbane, I train most days so the onsite gym is a genuine perk, and $1,000 in travel credits to a person who'd rather be on a trail somewhere is dangerously well targeted.
